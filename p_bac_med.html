Bacetrial-Mediated Material System

Engineering E.Coli to sense heavy-metal (Copper ions) for GFP expressions

The indigenous communities are threatened by capitalist ruins, such as the copper mining in Arizona, which has severely damaged local water resources. Within these water sources, accumulations of copper ions have emerged as a threat when exceeding allowed volumes.

As a design technologist, I aim to incorporate synthetic biology to create a bacterial sensing system cultivated in E.Coli. This bacterial system is then embedded in an additively manufactured material system that leverages the biomass waste streams. The outcome is a proof-of-concept framework that, in the form of a biodegradable planter, senses the heavy-metals in low-concentration water used by people and expresses designed protein reactions (GFP/Colorimetric) as visual indicators.

The syn-bio design is divided into the plasmid design and the wetlab execution. The plasmid design is based on a pesticide-sensing plasmid (FL030) provided by Prof. Ariel L. Furst of the MIT Furst Lab. The design process includes primer designs and Golden Gate Assembly as the major molecular cloning strategy.

The material system is built with an existing technology developed by Nic Lee of the MIT Media Lab. By mixing chitosan, cellulose, and organic waste material from destinated indigenous lands (coffee ground), the material host is 3d-printed based on a generative growth algorithm, the differential growth, computationally designed to maximize surface areas for bacterial accumulations and growth.

Societal Urgency

Indigenous Lands Occupied by the Morenci copper mine in Arizona

Phelps Dodge is now developing the Safford Mine, about 12 km southwest of Morenci. It will be the first new copper mine in the US in more than 30 years. When production starts in 2008, the Safford Mine will produce 109 thousand tons of copper.
