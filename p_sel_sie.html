Testing the “plus-shaped” model

Our initial plus-shaped design performed approximately as hypothesized: at rest, the prototype was fully open and permeable, and when force was applied to either axis the material would contract into a nearly solid surface along the corners of the negative shape between the pores. Using an Instron machine, we were able to a stress-strain data for both prototypes.

Test vs ideal depiction of “plus-shaped” ranging from fully open to fully shut.

Von Mises Stress simulations after max deformation of both designs

Building upon this first experiment, we modeled another prototype with rows of rotated plus shapes, as informed by the first Comsol simulation.

Results

This stress increased linearly until it reached an inflection point at which point the material began to deform and buckle in on itself, reducing the stress in the system. The strain continued to increase until the prototype reached its fully compressed state, at which point the stress then again increased linearly.

Comparing both designs in Stress- Strain data from Instron and COMSOL

The Instron testing of the improved “plus-shape” showed multiple inflection points on the stress-strain curve. This likely occurred because of the staggered buckling of rows, rather than uniform buckling at a single inflection point. Overall, the rotated plus shapes were much more resistant to compression than the uniform design.

Design speculations

While our initial vision was to solve the annoyance of a cluttered kitchen, this macro design could be scaled down to a smaller scale pore size and provide utility for broader level issues that require dynamic and precise filtration of different materials. These filters could be constructed from reusable, eco-friendly materials that could be easily cleaned in their rested state, and then compressed and turned to the appropriate pore size to filter water, air, or other particulates.
