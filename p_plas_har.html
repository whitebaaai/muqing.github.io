The Plastic Harvesters 2035

Recycling existing ocean plastics from the North Atlantic Garbage Patch through design reproductions

As the 2050-plastic-crisis approaches, how can we harvest existing ocean plastics and design new architectural artifacts that extend the life of plastic waste, developing new circular economic models?

In between 2020 and 2050, the project proposes a multi-scale mission. It reinvents plastic waste into recycled materials through architectural production, as an alternative method of reducing plastic debris in the ocean. “The Plastic Harvesters 2035” imagines a future where innovations in recycled materials emerge to save the Faroese economy during the 2050 ocean plastic crisis.

In a dystopian future, the North Atlantic Garbage Patch, the closest to the Arctic, would exert catastrophic effects on neighboring countries whose economies are highly reliant on ocean resources.

Proposed strategies include an industrial chain of ocean plastics, based on existing infrastructures of harvesting, transporting, manufacturing, distributing, and retailing. Ocean waste will be recycled as virgin materials to supple alternative design opportunities, especially when contextualized on cultural and technological grounds.

The film aims to encourage transformations of existing infrastructures in the remote ocean and advocate versatile designs opportunities with emerging eco-materials to build new markets for the design industry.

“The Exclusive Plastic Zone”

“The Exclusive Plastic Zone” (EPZ) is a proposed extension system of the Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ), as a way to raise international collaborations in cleaning the North Atlantic Garbage patch. In “Plastic Harvesters, 2035”, the EPZ is initiated by small island nations with mono-economies as a way to expand the variety of their economic incomes

The Cross-Scale Multi-Phase Strategy

GLOBAL SCALE

With legal and financial supports from emerging international policies, the Exclusive Plastic Zone (EEZ) is a system that treats the Atlantic Garbage patch as a recycling resource for each neighbouring country to collaboratively clean and reuse the ocean waste.

NATIONAL SCALE

The new policies will enable an established industrial chain, leading to the formation of a crafting culture. The artistic movement will then transform the existing artifacts, architectures, and infrastructures on the islands through sustainable design.

HUMAN SCALE

Local fishermen can work as waste harvesters and artisans that specialize in material innovations from ocean waste.

Converting Oil Rigs to Recycle Ocean Plastics

A development of the film “The Plastic Harvesters, 2035”.

Warming Competition 2020

Role: Concept design, 3D-Modeling, Rendering
Teammates: Xinyue Wang, Jingwei Qian

The project is centered around the following research question: While the ocean is scientifically proven the biggest CO2 depository on the planet, it is threatened by the soaring plastic pollutions to lose its capacity to keep storing carbon deposits from our energy-hungry lifestyle. To sustain the oceanic environment, how can we transform and re-adapt existing oceanic infrastructures towards ocean-plastic harvesting?

The ongoing global warming has triggered the birth of renewable technologies and aggressive government policies to replace the fossil fuel industries. With growing debts and the declining for fossil fuels, DIAMOND OFFSHORE, an offshore drilling company has recently filed for bankruptcy, liquidating existing oil drilling plants in the remote ocean that are due for suspended operations and decommissions. Our proposal to reconstruct the billion-dollar corporation, based on the real financial event speculates on a fiscally viable future in which the energy giants can contribute to humanity’s mission of environmental preservation and restorations.

By converting the oil rigs near the garbage patches into mobile plastic collection and conversion centers with minimal cost and structural changes, we aim to reconstruct the oceanic landscape as a synergetic community with the emerging ocean-plastic-harvesting industry, such as the OCEAN CLEANUP. The multi-functional collection-conversion center, featuring new storage spaces and processing capacities from the original rig would allow extended operational ranges for the OCEAN CLEANUP system.

Workers community on-site includes three major groups of workers; nomadic sailors on tankers, workers of DIAMOND OFFSHORE, outside workers from coastal countries
